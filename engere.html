Engere

Ethiopian Coffee Leaf Milk Brew
Engere is a documented South Ethiopian beverage made by combining coffee leaf brew with fresh cow's milk. The research records three household preparation types: simple, sweetened, and spiced.

Definition

What is Engere?

Engere is prepared by making a coffee leaf brew first, straining out the leaves, and then blending the hot strained brew with fresh cow's milk. Sweeteners, spices and herbs may be added depending on the household method.

It should be described as a traditional coffee leaf milk brew, not as ordinary tea, coffee, or a standalone dairy drink. Its cultural role is closer to a nourishing household beverage used for strength, recovery and daily support.

Careful wording: The study documents reported use and reported experience among households. It does not prove that Engere cures disease, increases milk production, or improves physical performance in a clinical trial.

Claims kept as supported

  • Engere is coffee leaf brew combined with fresh cow's milk.
  • The study documents three preparation types: simple, sweetened and spiced.
  • The reported preparation distribution is 19.7 percent simple, 22.6 percent sweetened and 37.7 percent spiced.
  • Engere is associated in the study with farmers, agro-pastoralists, lactating women, strength enhancement, recovery and medicinal use.
  • The study reports no self-reported gastritis or heart-related symptoms among Engere consumers, while plain coffee leaf brew had reported complaints among some respondents.

Claims softened or removed

  • The phrase "valued for centuries" has been softened because this page is based on a contemporary household study, not a direct historical chronology.
  • The claim that milk proves safety has been softened. The study reports respondent experience, not a clinical safety trial.
  • The casein and phenolic binding explanation is treated as a plausible interpretation, not as something directly proven by the Engere field paper.
  • The phrase "oldest documented coffee leaf culture" has been removed as an overclaim.
Best public wording: Engere has a strong reported safety profile in the Gofa Zone survey, but this should not be converted into a universal medical safety claim.

Simple Engere

  5. Add fresh cow's milk to the hot strained brew, stir gently and serve hot.

Spiced Engere, double pot
Leaf brew and spice brew are prepared separately, then combined and strained again.
  1. Pot 1: boil crushed coffee leaves in 1000 ml water for 7 to 15 minutes.
  2. Pot 2: boil spices and herbs separately in 500 ml water for 5 to 10 minutes.
  3. Strain the leaf brew into the spice brew. Discard the leaf solids.
  4. Boil the combined brew briefly for 2 to 3 minutes.
  5. Strain again to remove spice residue.
  6. Add 500 ml fresh cow's milk, sweeten to taste and serve hot.
The double pot method is a practical interpretation of separate extraction. It may give cleaner flavour separation, but the page should not imply that it is superior unless further sensory testing confirms it.
Use pattern

How Engere is consumed

  • Reported frequency among Engere preparing households: once daily, 55.6 percent; twice daily, 31.9 percent; three times daily, 12.5 percent.
  • Always served hot in the documented household practice.
  • Prepared fresh rather than stored as a ready to drink product.
  • Used by lactating women, farmers, labourers and households seeking a sustaining drink.
Interpretation

Why the milk matters

The field study reports a better self-reported tolerance pattern for Engere than for plain coffee leaf brew. The most careful interpretation is that milk may moderate the intensity of the brew and change its nutritional and sensory character.

It is plausible that milk proteins and fat alter bitterness, astringency, phenolic perception and gastric comfort. However, this mechanism should be presented as a food science explanation, not as a direct conclusion proven by the Engere household survey.
