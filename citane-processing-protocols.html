Citane: Processing & Protocols

Complete framework for coffee leaf processing pathways, flavor targeting, and detailed preparation methods

Overview: What is Citane Processing?

Citane processing is the systematic application of heat, fermentation, oxidation, and aging techniques to fresh coffee leaves—transforming them from perishable botanicals into shelf-stable, flavorful, functional beverages. Each processing pathway produces distinct sensory and chemical outcomes.

Citane is not a single method. It is a framework encompassing multiple approaches, each offering different flavor profiles, functional properties, and consumer positioning. The goal of this section is to map the complete landscape: what flavors are possible, how to achieve them, and what protocols ensure safety and reproducibility.

Unlike coffee roasting—which is well-documented globally—coffee leaf processing is only recently being systematized through scientific study. What follows combines peer-reviewed research protocols with documented traditional methods used in coffee-growing communities.

Core Processing Pathways

Coffee leaf can be processed along multiple distinct pathways, each emphasizing different characteristics:

Fresh & Light Pathways

Cold Fresh Citane: Enzyme-preserved, minimal oxidation. Bright, clean, grassy. Used fresh or briefly dried.

White Citane: Slow shade-wilting with controlled finishing. Honey-hay subtlety, premium positioning, maximum calmness compounds.

Oxidative Pathways

Oolong Citane: Wilt + bruise + partial oxidation. Layered complexity, floral-to-toasty progression. Most versatile pathway.

Roasted Citane: Post-dry roasting for deeper flavor. Comfort, warmth, niacin enhancement, cereal body.

Black-Style Citane: Heavy bruising, deep oxidation. Malty, raisin-like richness, full body.

Fermented Pathways

Lactic Fermented Citane: Tangy-sweet, gut-friendly functional appeal. Bridges fresh and aged profiles.

Koji-Assisted Citane: Umami depth, savory novel profile. Specialized technique.

Yeast-Fermented Citane: Fruity esters, craft-cider complexity. Experimental.

Advanced Pathways

Aged Citane (Puerh-Style): Compressed, aged over years. Deep earthy notes, profound complexity.

Smoked Citane: Light or heavy smoke exposure. Pine-resin or char-caramel notes.

Fire-Kissed Citane: Quick charcoal exposure. Mild char, caramel, roasted depth.

Each pathway can be customized further through leaf age selection, harvest timing, equipment choices, and finishing techniques. Together, they form a ladder of expression: from core styles to premium reserves to experimental expressions.

Citane Process Compass: Target Six Flavor Profiles

Instead of following a single pathway, Citane production can be goal-driven: choose your desired taste outcome, then follow the specific steps that produce it. Each target flavor is driven by specific volatile compounds that can be systematically produced through controlled processing.

Target 1: Honey-Like Sweetness

Driver: β-ionone (OAV 132–927)
Leaf choice: Older or yellow leaves (higher β-ionone potential)
Processing: Rolling or cutting before drying to enhance enzymatic release
Drying method: Sun drying (≥48 hours) or wild/yeast fermentation
Timeline: Wither 12h @ ~20°C → Ferment 12h @ ~25°C (yeast/Lactobacillus) → Sun dry ≥48h
Target markers: Honey-like aroma, measurable β-ionone impact >100 OAV

Target 2: Floral & Perfumed

Drivers: α-ionone (OAV 30–100), decanal (OAV 14–301)
Leaf choice: Young or mixed leaves (ionones linked to fresher tissues)
Processing: Rolling or freezing to rupture cells, then allow partial oxidation
Drying method: Sun drying or yeast fermentation to enhance floral volatiles
Timeline: Wither 12h → Ferment 12h → Sun dry ≥48h
Target markers: Perceptible floral bouquet, α-ionone and decanal among dominant volatiles

Target 3: Citrus-Like Freshness

Driver: Decanal (OAV up to 301), (E)-2-nonenal (OAV 1–11)
Leaf choice: Older leaves or roasted samples
Drying method: Oven drying (70°C for 4h) or roasting for more citrus-like aldehydes
Timeline: Wither 12h → Oven dry 4h at 70°C
Target markers: Crisp citrus aroma, decanal present at high OAV

Target 4: Fruity / Melon-Like

Drivers: Octanal (OAV 7–23), 2,4-nonadienal (OAV 2–18)
Processing: Rolling/cutting to stimulate enzymatic activity
Drying method: Sun drying for slow transformation + yeast fermentation (12h, 25°C)
Timeline: Wither 12h → Ferment 12h → Sun dry ≥48h
Target markers: Melon-like sweetness, octanal + 2,4-nonadienal perceptible in aroma profile

Target 5: Cucumber-Like Freshness

Driver: (E,Z)-2,6-nonadienal (OAV 18–256)
Leaf choice: Young leaves or shoots
Processing: Steaming (pot steaming until lid @ ~100°C) to preserve green freshness and deactivate enzymes
Drying method: Oven drying for clean profile
Timeline: Wither 12h → Steam briefly → Oven dry 4h at 70°C
Target markers: Clean cucumber aroma, strong (E,Z)-2,6-nonadienal expression

Target 6: Grassy / Green

Drivers: Hexanal (OAV 1–10), 4-heptenal (OAV 1–9)
Leaf choice: Young leaves with minimal processing
Processing: Blending or cutting to release grassy aldehydes
Drying method: Oven drying (fast, 70°C, 4h) locks in the green profile
Timeline: Wither 12h → Blend/cut → Oven dry 4h
Target markers: Fresh-cut grass aroma, hexanal clearly detectable
Standard brewing for all targets: 2g dried leaf in 200ml boiling water, infuse 5 minutes, remove leaves, cool briefly before serving.

Complete Processing Protocols

The following five protocols are adapted from peer-reviewed research and documented traditional methods. Each produces distinct flavor and functional outcomes.

Protocol 1: Non-Fermented Control (Green/Vegetal Profile)

Purpose: Produce baseline green leaf character with minimal oxidation. Reference style for comparison.

Expected flavor: Green/vegetal notes, mild bitterness, grassy aroma.

  • Harvest mature coffee leaves (3–6 months old)
  • Wash gently in clean water and surface-dry on cloth
  • Cut into 1–2 cm strips for even drying
  • Oven-dry at 60–70°C in ventilated oven (1–2 hours) until crisp
  • Cool completely and transfer to airtight container away from light

Brewing: 2g dried leaf in 200ml water @ 95°C; steep 7–8 minutes; strain.

Protocol 2: Fermented (Fruity/Sweet/Floral Profile)

Purpose: Develop fruity, honey-like, and floral volatiles through controlled fermentation.

Expected flavor: Honey-like, fruity, floral; softer green notes compared to non-fermented.

  • Harvest mature coffee leaves (3–6 months old)
  • Wash gently in clean water and surface-dry
  • Bruise leaves by rolling or crushing to break cell walls (critical step)
  • Ferment in closed container with damp cloth at 25–30°C for 12–24 hours
  • Leaves will darken and develop fruity aroma (sign of successful fermentation)
  • Oven-dry at 60–70°C until crisp and stable
  • Cool and store in airtight container away from light

Brewing: 2g dried fermented leaf in 200ml water @ 95°C; steep 6–7 minutes; strain.

Protocol 3: Mild Acidity (Bridging Style)

Purpose: Bridge gap between green/vegetal and fruity/fermented styles. Creates balanced cup with gentle acidity.

Expected flavor: Honey-like, fruity, with mild lactic or citrusy acidity (refreshing, not sharp).

  • Harvest mature green coffee leaves
  • Wash gently and surface-dry
  • Pretreatment (choose one): Either wither in shade 6–12 hours until pliable, OR freeze overnight (−18°C) and thaw
  • Bruise leaves firmly by rolling or crushing to break down cell walls
  • Ferment in closed container at 25–30°C for 18–24 hours
  • Optional: Add 1 tsp natural whey or sourdough water as starter to encourage lactic acid bacteria
  • Leaves should darken and develop fruity/floral aroma with mild tang
  • Oven-dry at 60–70°C until crisp and stable
  • Store in airtight container away from light and moisture

Brewing: 2–3g dried leaf in 200ml hot water (95°C); steep 6–8 minutes; strain.

Use case: Run side-by-side tastings against non-fermented control and full-fermented fruity style to determine optimal Citane positioning.

Protocol 4: Mature Leaf — Withering Route

Purpose: Soften tough mature leaves and develop rounded flavor. Withering allows gradual enzyme activity and sugar concentration.

Best for: Older or fibrous leaves that require pre-treatment for extractability.

Expected flavor: Softer infusion, more rounded and full-bodied, with fruity/honey notes.

  • Harvest mature green leaves
  • Wash gently and surface-dry
  • Wither in shaded, ventilated space for 6–12 hours until pliable
  • Roll or crush leaves firmly to break down cell walls
  • Ferment in closed container at 25–30°C for 12–24 hours
  • Leaves should darken and develop fruity aromas
  • Oven-dry at 60–70°C until crisp and stable
  • Store in airtight container away from light and moisture

Brewing: 2–3g dried leaf in 200ml water (95°C); steep 6–8 minutes; strain.

Distinction: Creates roundness and smoothness; compare with freezing route for sensory difference.

Protocol 5: Mature Leaf — Freezing (Cryo-Treatment) Route

Purpose: Disrupt rigid cell walls through ice-crystal formation, then ferment to unlock sweetness and aroma. Creates cleaner, brighter profile.

Best for: Tough mature leaves requiring maximum extractability.

Expected flavor: Cleaner fruity/floral notes with enhanced extractability. May preserve more sweetness than withering route.

  • Harvest mature green leaves
  • Wash gently and surface-dry
  • Freeze in freezer (−18°C) overnight to break cell structure through ice crystals
  • Allow to thaw at room temperature until pliable
  • Roll or crush leaves thoroughly after thawing
  • Ferment in closed container at 25–30°C for 12–24 hours
  • Fruity/floral aroma should develop clearly
  • Oven-dry at 60–70°C until crisp and stable
  • Store in airtight container away from light and moisture

Brewing: 2–3g dried leaf in 200ml water (95°C); steep 6–8 minutes; strain.

Recommendation: Run side-by-side trials comparing withering (roundness) vs freezing (clarity) to identify your preferred sensory outcome.

Sheng-Style Citane: Dry Aging Protocol

Sheng-style Citane is dry-aged coffee leaf, inspired by Pu'erh Sheng tea. Leaves are made shelf-stable first (sanitized, dried to low moisture), then allowed to age naturally under controlled storage. Changes occur through slow chemical reactions (oxidation, polymerization), not active fermentation.

Result: A mellower, rounder, deeper Citane cup that evolves over months and years. Reserve/premium product positioning.

Materials Needed

  • Fresh coffee leaves (healthy, tender, pest-free)
  • Clean cotton cloth (unscented) for lining and wrapping
  • Wooden planks or screw press for shaping
  • Combi oven or equivalent (steam + dehydration modes)
  • Weights or clamps for pressing
  • Paper or cotton pouches for wrapping
  • Clean cotton bags for storage
  • Storage environment: 20–25°C, 60–70% RH, odor-free
  • Labels and logbook for batch tracking

Step-by-Step Protocol

  • 1. Harvest & Sort: Select tender, healthy leaves. Remove damaged or insect-bitten material.
  • 2. Wilt: Allow leaves to soften in shade or combi (low heat, gentle airflow) until pliable.
  • 3. Steam (Sanitize & Soften): Briefly steam leaves in combi at ~100°C for 1–3 minutes. Purpose: sanitize surface and make leaves pliable for pressing.
  • 4. Press (Form Cakes): Place steamed leaves between cloth, sandwich with planks, and weigh down or use screw press. Keep pressed for 24–48 hours (not longer, to avoid damp mold risk).
  • 5. Dry / Dehydrate: Transfer cakes to combi at 55–60°C, low humidity, gentle fan. Dry until crisp, papery, and pass the "snap test" (leaves break cleanly).
  • 6. Trim & Wrap: Trim rough edges if needed. Wrap cakes in plain paper or cotton pouches.
  • 7. Store / Age: Place wrapped cakes inside cotton bags. Store at 20–25°C, 60–70% RH, odor-free, clean environment. Monitor monthly for aroma, dryness, and appearance.

Aging Timeline

0–3 Months
Flavors settle, bitterness softens. Immediate post-production freshness gives way to subtle mellowing.
6 Months
First noticeable divergence from fresh Citane; smoother, deeper amber liquor. Rounding begins to become apparent in the cup.
12 Months
Clear aged character evident. Honey-woody aroma, mellow taste. Distinct from fresh Citane; approaching premium maturity.
2–3 Years
Rich, earthy-mineral complexity. Deeper body. Aging effects pronounced. Complex layering in the cup.
5+ Years
Fully matured, smooth, profound but with loss of brightness. Reserve expression with significant aging depth.

Safety Notes

  • Always ensure leaves are fully dried before long-term storage (snap test is critical)
  • Cakes must be crisp and papery, not pliable
  • Use breathable wraps (paper, cotton) — avoid airtight plastic
  • Discard any cakes showing visible mold or musty odor
  • Keep batches small and labeled with date for full traceability
  • Monitor storage environment: 20–25°C, 60–70% relative humidity are optimal ranges

Citane Expression: From Core to Reserve

The protocols and pathways outlined above form a ladder of expression for Citane:

Core Styles: Non-fermented, fermented, and mild acidity protocols. These are the foundational expressions—accessible, consistent, straightforward.

Premium Expressions: Single-target flavor pathways (honey-sweet, floral, citrus-fresh, etc.). These require more technical control and ingredient specification but deliver distinct sensory experiences.

Experimental Reserves: Sheng-style aged Citane, smoked variants, koji-fermented, yeast-fermented. These push the boundaries of what Citane can be. They require investment in aging infrastructure and specialized techniques.

Each pathway can be developed into a unique product story—each with its own positioning, target consumer, and price point.

Using These Protocols

These protocols are designed for:

Estate producers wanting to systematically develop Citane products from their leaves.

Home practitioners experimenting with different processing approaches.

Commercial operations seeking standardized, reproducible methods.

For each protocol:

Start with the non-fermented control. This gives you a baseline understanding of your leaf material and equipment.

Then try the fermented version. Compare them side-by-side. Notice the differences in flavor, body, and aroma.

Target one specific flavor outcome. Use the Citane Process Compass to choose a target (e.g., honey-sweetness) and follow that specific pathway.

Document everything. Record leaf age, harvest date, fermentation time and temperature, drying time and temperature, tasting notes. Patterns will emerge.

Iterate. Each batch teaches you something. Refine your technique based on results.

Through this systematic approach, you move from following recipes to understanding the principles. That understanding allows you to adapt protocols to your specific conditions, leaf material, equipment, and desired outcomes.
