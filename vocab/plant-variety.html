Plant Variety & Anatomy

Genetics · Structure · What the Leaf Gives

The plant is the foundation of flavour, function, and character. Variety type and leaf anatomy together determine what the leaf is capable of becoming.

Leaf Origin Index Plant Care
Citane Context — Thumpassery Estate

Citane uses Arabica Chandragiri — an improved variety selected for performance in Kerala's lowland, rubber-shaded conditions. Its leaf anatomy and compound profile reflect both its genetics and its growing environment.

Plant Variety Types
Wild (Landrace)
Variety Type

Naturally occurring coffee plants with high genetic diversity. Not selected for commercial production. Carry unique local character and important reference points for understanding species potential.

None →
Heirloom
Variety Type

Traditional varieties passed down through generations. Adapted to their local environment, often complex in flavour, resilient without external inputs. Carry the memory of a place and practice.

None →
Improved Variety
Variety Type

Selected for specific qualities — yield, disease resistance, consistency. Citane uses Arabica Chandragiri, an improved variety for Kerala conditions. Trades some genetic diversity for predictability.

None →
Clonal
Variety Type

Propagated vegetatively from a single parent to ensure genetic uniformity. Consistent character batch to batch. Reduces genetic resilience over time.

None →
Hybrid
Variety Type

The result of crossing two distinct varieties to combine desirable traits. First-generation hybrids are often vigorous; maintaining traits across generations requires careful management.

None →
Future Varieties
Variety Type

Varieties currently in development — bred for climate resilience and quality. As growing conditions shift, future variety development is essential for long-term coffee leaf production.

Leaf Anatomy
Apical Bud
Leaf Part

The youngest growth at the branch tip. Highest in certain bioactive compounds. Most tender and most delicate to handle. In some preparations, the most prized part of the plant.

None →
Young Leaf
Leaf Part

Leaves immediately below the apical bud. Tender, rich in active compounds, bright in flavour character, more perishable than mature leaves.

None →
Mature Leaf
Leaf Part

Fully expanded leaf with complete structure. Balanced flavours, more body, denser compound profile. More robust and forgiving in processing.

None →
Leaf Stem (Petiole)
Leaf Part

The short stalk connecting leaf blade to branch. Carries water and nutrients. In processing, stems are sometimes removed to alter extraction behaviour; in others, retained as part of whole-leaf character.

None →
What the Leaf Gives
Flavour
Leaf Contribution

A complex interplay of aroma, taste, body, and finish. Coffee leaf flavour is distinct from both coffee and tea — shaped by its own compound profile and its own processing logic.

See Flavour & Aroma → Research
Bioactives
Leaf Contribution

Polyphenols, chlorogenic acids, mangiferin, caffeine, GABA, and other compounds present in the leaf. Profiles vary by variety, altitude, leaf age, and processing method.

None →
Identity
Leaf Contribution

The leaf as an expression of place, people, and tradition. Citane's identity as a rubber-shaded lowland Kerala leaf is unique — no other producer can replicate it.
