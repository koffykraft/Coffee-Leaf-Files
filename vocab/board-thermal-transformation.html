The Buna Thermal Principle — Not Tea Logic

In conventional tea, extended wet heat increases bitterness and astringency. In coffee leaf, extended wet heat increases sweetness and decreases bitterness. This is the single most important thermal fact for anyone preparing Buna. The Kuti tradition of extended decoction, and the Engere milk boil, both rely on this principle. Do not apply tea logic to Buna thermal work.

Our Principle

Apply the right heat, for the right time, at the right stage — to bring out the best, and preserve the rest. Right heat. Right time. Right care. Right result.

Wet Heat Stages

Water or milk as the medium · Temperature determines extraction rate and flavour development
WH 1
〰️ Light Simmer ~70–80°C

Barely moving liquid. Small bubbles forming slowly. For delicate preparations where aromatic volatiles must be preserved and extraction is intentionally slow. Preserves green and floral notes.

Gentle aromatic extraction
WH 2
🌊 Simmer ~85–90°C

Steady movement, regular bubbles. The standard heat stage for most Buna decoction preparations. Compound release is steady and controlled. Sweetness begins to develop.

Balanced extraction
WH 3
💨 Strong Simmer ~90–95°C

Active agitation approaching boil. Faster extraction, fuller body, beginning concentration through evaporation. Sweetness increases; bitterness remains low in good leaf.

Fuller body, more sweetness
WH 4
♨️ Long Boil 100°C, extended

The Engere and Kuti traditional stage. Extended boiling in water or milk. Sweetness peaks, bitterness decreases. Colour deepens. A brothy, full-bodied character develops. This is where Buna diverges most clearly from tea.

Maximum sweetness · Traditional
WH 5
🫙 Reduction Sustained boil

Volume decreases through evaporation, concentrating all compounds. Used for syrups, concentrates, culinary Buna bases. Quality of the base extraction determines quality of the reduction — concentration amplifies both virtues and faults.

Concentration · Culinary use

Dry Heat Stages — Roasting

No water medium · Maillard reaction and caramelisation · Applied to dried leaf
DH 1
🌿 Light Roast Early Maillard

Gentle dry heat. Toast and hay notes develop. Green character still dominant. Leaf remains visibly pale. Increases shelf stability without significantly altering the fresh leaf flavour profile.

Green + toast · Shelf stable
DH 2
🌾 Medium Roast Caramel development

Moderate dry heat. Caramel, nutty, toasted grain notes develop. Leaf visibly darker. Green character partially replaced by roasted compounds. Most versatile thermal treatment — balances fresh and developed character.

Balance of green + roasted
DH 3
🟫 Dark Roast Cacao development

High dry heat. Cacao, woody, deep notes dominate. Green and floral notes largely lost. Produces a full-bodied cup with significant roasted character. Requires careful control to avoid crossing into char.

Roasted · Full body
DH 4
⬛ Char / Toast Boundary condition

The limit of dry heat. Intentional in some smoked traditional preparations (Kawa Daun adjacent). Otherwise signals a processing error. Understanding this boundary is essential for consistent quality control.

Boundary · Handle with care

Temperature Control & Cooling

After heat · Preserving what was created
❄️ Cooling Ambient airflow

Allowing leaf or liquid to return to ambient temperature, stopping residual heat from continuing to transform the product. Cooling must be complete before packaging or storing. Warm leaf in a sealed container traps steam and degrades quality.

Protect aroma · Protect texture
🧊 Chilling Rapid temperature drop

Rapidly reducing temperature of a brewed extraction. Crash chilling after hot extraction preserves aromatic volatiles that would otherwise dissipate slowly. Essential step for cold brew and iced Buna preparation.

Lock in aroma · Cold service
🗄️ Cold Storage Refrigeration

Holding prepared extractions at refrigeration temperature to slow degradation. Dried leaf does not require cold storage if moisture is correctly managed. Prepared liquid extractions do. A quality decision, not a default.

Extend extraction quality
🌡️ Warm Hold Controlled temperature

Maintaining a prepared brew at a specific elevated temperature for service. For decoction-style preparations served over extended periods. Temperature and duration must be defined to prevent continued extraction or quality loss.
