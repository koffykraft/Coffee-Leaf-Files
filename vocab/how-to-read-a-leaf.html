How to Read a Leaf

Citane Field Framework · Between Plant and Process

Before the leaf enters any processing pathway, it already carries information. Its age, origin, health, season, condition, and intended use determine what is possible.

Seasons & Phenology Index Quality at Source
Citane Context — Thumpassery Estate

Chandragiri Arabica grown beneath mature rubber canopy at approximately 130 m elevation in Karavaloor, Kerala. A lowland, shade-grown, warm-climate leaf. These six questions were developed specifically for this leaf and this context. They may need adjustment for highland or sun-grown material.

Why this page exists

Most agricultural systems begin with a process. Citane begins with a question. A leaf that is understood can be processed with purpose. A leaf that is not understood is processed by assumption.

This framework was developed at Thumpassery Estate as a practical method for understanding coffee leaves before processing. It is not borrowed from tea. It is not borrowed from coffee roasting. It emerged from repeated observation of coffee leaves as their own material.

The Citane Reading — Six Questions
Question 1 — How old is it?
Age · Leaf Stage

Age determines potential. Flush, young, intermediate, mature, and old leaves are not simply different sizes of the same material. They represent different chemical and structural states. Younger leaves generally contain higher concentrations of certain bioactive compounds and possess more delicate structure. Mature leaves tend to withstand handling and heat more effectively. Without age, there is no context.

See Leaf Stages → Research
Question 2 — Where did it grow?
Origin · Terroir

Origin establishes the baseline. A leaf grown in deep shade is not the same as a leaf grown in full sun. A lowland leaf is not the same as a highland leaf. A leaf produced during a wet season is not identical to one produced during a dry period. Before processing begins, the environment has already shaped the leaf.

See Leaf Origin → Research
Question 3 — Was it healthy?
Health · Stress

Health reveals hidden history. The plant records its experiences in the leaf. Nutrient stress, drought, disease, pest pressure, excessive shade, excessive sun, and physical damage all leave traces. A healthy leaf is not merely attractive. It is structurally intact, chemically balanced, and predictable during processing. The cup often reveals what the eye ignored.

See Plant Care → Observed
Question 4 — What season produced it?
Seasonality · Phenology

Time shapes character. Leaves produced during periods of rapid growth often differ from leaves produced during slower growth. Rainfall, temperature, humidity, and growth rate influence both the leaf itself and the conditions under which it must later be processed. Season is not background information. Season is an active ingredient.

See Seasons & Phenology → Research
Question 5 — What condition is it in now?
Harvest Condition · Immediate Suitability

Harvest condition determines immediate possibilities. Fresh, wilted, bruised, damaged, weathered, or partially dehydrated leaves do not respond identically. The same leaf may require a different pathway depending on its condition at the moment it reaches the processing table. Age describes what the leaf became. Condition describes what has happened to it since.

See Quality at Source → Observed
Question 6 — What do you want from it?
Processing Intent · Target

Intent guides transformation. No leaf possesses a single destiny. The same harvest may become a green-style preparation, an oxidised preparation, a roasted preparation, or a decoction-oriented product. The objective is not to discover one correct answer. The objective is to select a pathway that respects both the leaf and the desired outcome.

See Process — Shaping the Leaf → Observed

The principle behind the reading

These six questions can be completed in less than two minutes. Together they establish: Age · Origin · Health · Season · Condition · Intent.

Every later decision — whether to wither, oxidise, steam, roast, dry, ferment, or decoct — becomes easier once these six dimensions are understood.

The framework is deliberately simple. Its purpose is not to replace observation. Its purpose is to organise observation.

A Citane perspective

In tea, the ideal harvest is often defined before processing begins. In coffee roasting, the green bean arrives as a largely fixed material. Buna occupies a different position.

The leaf remains open to multiple futures. The leaf and the process influence one another. The harvest and the intention are negotiated together.

This conversation between leaf and process is one of the foundations of the Citane project.

Every preparation begins with the same act: read the leaf.
