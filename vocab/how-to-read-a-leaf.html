How to Read a Leaf

Citane Field Framework · Between Plant and Process

Before the leaf enters any processing pathway, six questions determine what is possible. This page is not borrowed from tea. It is not borrowed from coffee. It belongs to Buna.

Seasons & Phenology Index Quality at Source
Citane Context — Thumpassery Estate

Chandragiri Arabica grown beneath mature rubber canopy at approximately 130 m elevation in Karavaloor, Kerala. A lowland, shade-grown, warm-climate leaf. These six questions were developed specifically for this leaf and this context. They may need adjustment for highland or sun-grown material.

Why this page exists

Every processing decision — what heat to apply, how long to wither, whether to oxidise or arrest — depends on the state of the leaf at the moment of harvest. A leaf that is answered correctly through these six questions arrives at processing with its potential understood. A leaf that arrives unread is processed by assumption, not by knowledge.

Six Questions — The Citane Leaf Reading
Question 1 — How old is it?
Leaf Age · Stage

Tender flush, young leaf, intermediate, mature, or old? Age determines compound profile, enzyme activity, and which processing pathways are open. Young leaves carry higher chlorogenic acid concentrations and more delicate volatile potential. Mature leaves carry more tannins and withstand heat better.

See Leaf Stages → Research
Question 2 — Where did it grow?
Origin · Terroir

Sun or shade? High or low altitude? Wet or dry season growth? The growing conditions shape the chemical baseline of the leaf before any processing begins. At Thumpassery, rubber shade at 130 m produces a specific compound profile that differs from highland or open-canopy material.

See Leaf Origin → Research
Question 3 — Was it healthy?
Plant Health · Stress

Stressed leaves — from disease, water deficit, nutrient deficiency, or pest damage — carry a different compound profile and behave differently under processing. A healthy leaf has intact cellular structure, uniform colour, and no off-aromas at harvest. What cannot be seen at harvest will appear in the cup.

See Plant Care → Observed
Question 4 — Wet season or dry season?
Seasonality · Phenology

Rainy season leaves are typically higher in water content, faster growing, and more delicate. Dry season leaves are denser and more concentrated. Season affects both the leaf's intrinsic character and the ambient conditions during processing — humidity, temperature, and drying rate all shift.

See Seasons & Phenology → Research
Question 5 — Tender or mature?
Texture · Processing Suitability

Tender leaves require gentler processing — lower heat, shorter withering, less mechanical disruption. Mature leaves tolerate more. The question is not which is better, but which processing pathway suits the leaf in hand. Mismatching leaf maturity to process produces poor results regardless of skill.

See Process → Observed
Question 6 — What do you want from it?
Processing Intent · Target

Green and fresh? Fermented and complex? Decocted and sweet? Roasted and deep? The leaf's potential does not dictate the target — the target dictates which of the leaf's potential to unlock. Reading the leaf is only half the work. Knowing where you are going is the other half.

See Process — Shaping the Leaf → Observed

The Reading in Practice

These six questions take less than two minutes at harvest. Run through them in sequence. Note the answers. They become the first entry in the batch record — and the context within which every subsequent processing decision is made. A leaf well-read at harvest is a leaf that processing can honour.

  • Age — flush / young / intermediate / mature / old
  • Origin — shade/sun · season · altitude · block
  • Health — uniform colour / intact / no off-aroma
  • Season — wet / dry / transition
  • Texture — tender / firm / mature / stiff
  • Target — green / oolong / black / decoction / roast

This is not tea logic. It is not coffee logic.

In tea, the answer to Question 1 (flush, two leaves) is fixed. In coffee roasting, Question 6 (roast profile) is fixed. In Buna, both are open variables. The leaf and the intent are negotiated together. That negotiation — specific to this plant, this place, and this practice — is the beginning of the Citane framework.
