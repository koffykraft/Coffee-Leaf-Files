The Primer

If you grow coffee, run a café, or simply want to understand what Citane is and why it behaves the way it does — start here. This section explains every key idea in plain language before the full technical paper begins.[span_5](start_span)[span_5](end_span)

Primer Section 01: What This Document Is — And What It Is Not

This document is a thinking tool. It is not a recipe, a processing protocol, or a guarantee of any flavour outcome. It is a framework for understanding the coffee leaf as a biochemical system — and for making sense of what happens when that system is put through different kinds of processing.[span_6](start_span)[span_6](end_span)

Think of it like a map of a territory that has not yet been fully explored. The map is drawn from what science already knows about similar territories — tea processing, fermented foods, plant chemistry — applied carefully and honestly to the coffee leaf. Some parts of the map are well-grounded. Others are speculative. Where we are speculating, we say so clearly.[span_7](start_span)[span_7](end_span)

Who Should Read This

Farmers: Understanding what is chemically active in your leaf, and when, will help you make better decisions about when to harvest, how to handle the leaf immediately after picking, and what processing choices are worth experimenting with.[span_8](start_span)[span_8](end_span)

Café owners: Understanding the chemistry behind different Citane processing styles will help you describe what you are serving accurately, choose products that match your menu, and ask better questions of your suppliers.[span_9](start_span)[span_9](end_span)

Processors: This is your core reference. The Reservoir Model in Part II is the most directly practical section for your day-to-day decisions.[span_10](start_span)[span_10](end_span)

The full technical paper follows this primer. It is comprehensive, detailed, and references the scientific literature. You do not need to read it all to understand the core idea — the primer covers what you need. But if you want the reasoning behind any specific claim, the full paper has it.[span_11](start_span)[span_11](end_span)

Primer Section 02: What Is Inside Your Leaf — In Plain Language

A coffee leaf is not a simple thing. Under a microscope, it is a dense ecosystem of molecules — dozens of different chemical compounds, each stored in different parts of the leaf cell, each waiting for a specific trigger to become active.[span_12](start_span)[span_12](end_span)

Scientists at the University of Siena analysed Coffea arabica leaves in detail and found 39 distinct compounds. These fall into several families. Here is what each family is, in plain language:[span_13](start_span)[span_13](end_span)

FamilyDescription
Chlorogenic AcidsThe most abundant compounds in the leaf by weight. They contribute structure, a clean acidity, and mild bitterness to the brewed cup. They are also the primary driver of antioxidant activity. Under heat or oxidation, they transform into many different products.[span_14](start_span)[span_14](end_span)
MangiferinA distinctive compound found in mango and, unusually, in coffee leaves. It is rare in beverage ingredients. Its flavour role in a brewed cup is not yet fully understood — which makes it one of the most interesting compounds in the leaf.[span_15](start_span)[span_15](end_span)
CatechinsThe same family of compounds that makes green tea behave the way it does. When exposed to air and enzymes, catechins oxidise — creating the amber colour and rounded, tea-like flavour associated with oolong and black tea. Coffee leaves contain them too.[span_16](start_span)[span_16](end_span)
ProcyanidinsLarger, more complex polyphenol structures related to catechins. Found in red wine, dark chocolate, and apple skins. They contribute body and a structured mouthfeel. Coffee leaves contain a range of procyanidin sizes — from dimers to tetramers.[span_17](start_span)[span_17](end_span)
FlavonoidsA broad family of plant pigments and protective compounds. Many are bound to sugar molecules (glycosides) that lock them in an inactive form. Processing can release the active molecule from this lock.[span_18](start_span)[span_18](end_span)
Amino AcidsThe building blocks of protein, but in small free-floating amounts. Not flavour compounds themselves — but critical flavour precursors. Under heat, amino acids react with sugars to produce toasted, nutty, caramel, and roasted aromas (this is called the Maillard reaction).[span_19](start_span)[span_19](end_span)
SugarsBoth free sugars and sugars attached to other molecules. They are the energy currency of the leaf — used by microbes during fermentation, by heat during browning, and by enzymes during breakdown of glycosides.[span_20](start_span)[span_20](end_span)
Caffeine & TrigonellineThe two alkaloids. Caffeine is well known. Trigonelline is less famous but equally interesting — it degrades under roasting to produce the distinctive pyridine notes of roasted coffee. Coffee leaves contain significant quantities of both.[span_21](start_span)[span_21](end_span)
ChlorophyllThe green pigment that powers photosynthesis. In processing terms, chlorophyll is both a colour indicator and a flavour precursor. A green leaf smells grassy and vegetal. A withered, oxidised, or roasted leaf smells very different — largely because chlorophyll has transformed.[span_22](start_span)[span_22](end_span)
LignansUnusual compounds found in two forms in the coffee leaf (cinchonain isomers). These were not previously documented in coffee leaves. Their flavour role is unknown — but their presence is scientifically significant.[span_23](start_span)[span_23](end_span)
Lipids (Fats)Coffee leaves contain far less fat than coffee beans, but not zero. Even small amounts matter. When plant lipids are broken down by enzymes or heat, they produce some of the most recognisable aromas in food: the fresh, grassy smell of a cut leaf, and deeper roasted or cooked notes under heat. This pool has not been studied in coffee leaves specifically.[span_24](start_span)[span_24](end_span)
Volatile PrecursorsMany of the most interesting aroma compounds in a processed leaf were not present in the raw leaf — they were created during processing. Volatile precursors are the locked or inactive parent molecules that become vivid aromas when unlocked. Some are terpene compounds locked to sugar molecules. Others are carotenoid pigments that release floral and tobacco-adjacent volatiles under heat. This is theoretically one of the most exciting and least explored areas of Citane chemistry.[span_25](start_span)[span_25](end_span)
The Simple Version

Your leaf is full of locked potential. Most of these compounds do nothing until something activates them — heat, air, water, time, or microbes. The art of Citane processing is choosing which locks to open, in which order, and for how long.[span_26](start_span)[span_26](end_span)

Primer Section 03: A Glossary of Terms — Words You Will Encounter

TermDefinition
ReservoirIn this document, a reservoir is a group of related compounds inside the leaf that behave similarly under processing. Instead of thinking about 39 individual molecules, we group them into 11 reservoirs — each with its own character and its own responses to different treatments.[span_27](start_span)[span_27](end_span)
Activation EventAnything that triggers chemical change in the leaf. Rolling, soaking, exposing to air, applying heat, allowing microbes to work, or simply waiting — each of these is an activation event. The leaf does not transform on its own. Something must trigger it.[span_28](start_span)[span_28](end_span)
Energy SystemThe underlying force that drives a chemical reaction. Heat provides thermal energy. Enzymes lower the energy needed for a reaction to occur. Microbes harvest energy from one compound and release it into another. Nothing happens in chemistry without an energy system behind it.[span_29](start_span)[span_29](end_span)
Process DomainA recognisable territory of chemistry — what happens when a particular set of activation events are combined. The Oxidative Domain (like black tea) is one. The Thermal Domain (like roasting) is another. Each domain produces its own characteristic sensory profile.[span_30](start_span)[span_30](end_span)
Emergent StateThe observable result of a processing sequence — the flavour territory your Citane ends up in. We describe five: Green (Alpha), Amber (Beta), Brown (Gamma), Roasted (Delta), and Unknown (Epsilon).[span_31](start_span)[span_31](end_span)
Maillard ReactionThe chemical reaction between amino acids and sugars under heat that creates toasted, nutty, caramel, and roasted aromas. It requires temperatures above approximately 120°C.[span_32](start_span)[span_32](end_span)
PPO (Polyphenol Oxidase)An enzyme present in most plant leaves that causes oxidative browning when the leaf is damaged — the same process that turns a cut apple brown. In tea processing, PPO drives the oxidation of catechins.[span_33](start_span)[span_33](end_span)
Glycoside / AglyconeA glycoside is an active compound locked to a sugar molecule, which makes it flavour-inactive. The aglycone is the active molecule freed from that sugar.[span_34](start_span)[span_34](end_span)
KojiAspergillus oryzae — a food-safe mould used in Japanese cooking to make sake, miso, and soy sauce. It produces a cocktail of enzymes that can transform the chemistry of a leaf at low temperatures without heat damage.[span_35](start_span)[span_35](end_span)
Route MapA record of a specific processing sequence and its observed outcome. If a batch produces something worth repeating, the route map is how you find your way back.[span_36](start_span)[span_36](end_span)
Precursor NetworkThe idea that compounds in the leaf are not isolated — they are connected. A sugar feeds a microbe. The microbe produces an acid. The compounds form a web, not a list.[span_37](start_span)[span_37](end_span)
CascadeA sequence of events where each step triggers the next: A → B → C → D. A wither is a cascade — moisture loss activates enzymes, which unlock aromas, which release sugars, which feed microbes.[span_38](start_span)[span_38](end_span)
Feedback LoopA cycle where a downstream product changes the conditions for an upstream reaction. Feedback loops are what make long processing sequences produce outcomes that surprise you — small effects that accumulate and amplify over time.[span_39](start_span)[span_39](end_span)

Primer Section 04: The Core Idea — In One Page

The leaf is not a finished ingredient. It is a system of potential, waiting to be directed. The compounds inside it do not have fixed flavours. They have possible flavours — depending on what you do to the leaf, and in what order.[span_40](start_span)[span_40](end_span)

There are three layers to understand:[span_41](start_span)[span_41](end_span)

  • 1

    Energy System → drives → Activation Event

    e.g., Heat (energy) drives Roasting (event)[span_42](start_span)[span_42](end_span)

  • 2

    Activation Event → unlocks → Reservoir(s)

    e.g., Rolling (event) unlocks the Catechin Reservoir via PPO[span_43](start_span)[span_43](end_span)

  • 3

    Reservoir → transforms into → Emergent State

    e.g., Oxidised catechins produce State Beta — the amber, tea-like profile[span_44](start_span)[span_44](end_span)

System Dynamics

The pipeline above is the skeleton of the model. But there is one more dimension: the reservoirs are not separate rooms. They interact. Sugars feed microbes. Microbes produce acids. Acids change how enzymes work. This web of connections — with its cascades and feedback loops — is what makes extended processing genuinely complex.[span_45](start_span)[span_45](end_span)

The five Emergent States are the possible flavour territories your Citane can arrive at:[span_46](start_span)[span_46](end_span)

  • α Green: Fresh, vegetal, herbal. Minimal processing.[span_47](start_span)[span_47](end_span)
  • β Amber: Rounded, tea-like, honeyed. Partial or full oxidation.[span_48](start_span)[span_48](end_span)
  • γ Brown: Complex, fermented, layered. Microbial activity has played a significant role.[span_49](start_span)[span_49](end_span)
  • δ Roasted: Nutty, toasted, coffee-adjacent. Deep thermal transformation.[span_50](start_span)[span_50](end_span)
  • ε Unknown: Multiple domains overlapping. This is where genuinely new Citane identities emerge.[span_51](start_span)[span_51](end_span)

Primer Section 05: Why This Matters — For Farmers and Café Owners

For the farmer: The leaf you harvest is not neutral. Its chemistry at the moment of harvest — shaped by altitude, shade, leaf age, season, and variety — determines what processing can do with it.[span_52](start_span)[span_52](end_span)

For the café owner: When you buy a Citane product, you are buying the result of a set of processing decisions — activation events applied to a specific leaf. Understanding the framework gives you a vocabulary for what you taste and why.[span_53](start_span)[span_53](end_span)

The One Thing to Remember

Every cup of Citane is the result of a journey through the leaf's chemistry. This document is a map of the possible journeys. No journey is inherently better than another. They lead to different places.[span_54](start_span)[span_54](end_span)

Part I — The Leaf as Laboratory

The scientific foundation. What the coffee leaf contains, how we know, and why the standard approach to beverage processing needs to be rethought for this ingredient.[span_55](start_span)[span_55](end_span)

Section 01: The Leaf Is Not a Finished Ingredient

Most beverage frameworks start from a known target. This document proposes a different starting point — one that is closer to how a chemist or plant biologist thinks about the material.[span_56](start_span)[span_56](end_span)

The coffee leaf is not a finished ingredient waiting to be prepared. It is a living biochemical reservoir that has been harvested at a particular moment in its metabolic life. Processing does not create these things. It redirects what is already there.[span_57](start_span)[span_57](end_span)

Central Premise

The leaf contains numerous precursor pools existing simultaneously. Different processing choices alter which pools become active and which remain dormant. The role of the Citane practitioner is not to reproduce a known flavour, but to navigate the landscape and record the routes taken.[span_58](start_span)[span_58](end_span)

Part II — The Reservoir Model

The coffee leaf is a collection of distinct chemical systems. We identify eleven reservoirs (A through K). Eight are directly evidenced by Cangeloni 2022; three — Sugar (I), Lipid (J), and Volatile Precursor (K) — are proposed theoretical extensions based on known plant leaf chemistry.[span_81](start_span)[span_81](end_span)

Section 04: Reservoir A — Chlorogenic Acid System

Reservoir A — The Dominant Pool

Compounds: 5-CGA (16.27 g/kg DW), 3-CGA, 4-CGA, 4,5-dCQA, 5-FQA, and Quinic acid.[span_82](start_span)[span_82](end_span)

Connections: Connects to Reservoir C (Catechin quinone browning coupling) and Reservoir D (Amino acids Maillard network interactions).[span_83](start_span)[span_83](end_span)

Behaviour: PPO oxidation yields amber/brown tones; thermal degradation above 160°C builds structured lactone bitterness layers.[span_84](start_span)[span_84](end_span)

Section 05: Reservoir B — Mangiferin & Xanthone System

Reservoir B — The Distinctive Pool

Compounds: Mangiferin (4.43 g/kg DW), Isomangiferin, Iriflophenone 3-C-glucoside, and 6-O-(p-hydroxybenzoyl)mangiferin.[span_85](start_span)[span_85](end_span)

Behaviour: High thermal stability. Serves as an exceptional antioxidant cushion that modifies how fast the adjacent catechin systems turn amber.[span_86](start_span)[span_86](end_span)

Section 06: Reservoir C — Catechin & Procyanidin System

Reservoir C — The Oxidation Engine

Compounds: Catechin/Epicatechin peaks, Procyanidin B dimer, Trimer A-type, Tetramer B-type, and Procyanidin C.[span_87](start_span)[span_87](end_span)

Behaviour: Under PPO-driven bruising environments, these polymerise into deep, structured components that develop classic tea-like density and body profiles.[span_88](start_span)[span_88](end_span)

Section 07: Reservoir D — Amino Acid System

Reservoir D — The Flavour Precursor Pool

Compounds: Alanine, Leucine, Glutamine, Aspartic acid, Cysteine, Malic acid, and Lactic acid.[span_89](start_span)[span_89](end_span)

Behaviour: High reactivity index. When coupled with sugar elements under dry heat, it converts into pyrazines and furans. Free pools are expanded via upstream protease work.[span_90](start_span)[span_90](end_span)

Section 08: Reservoir E — Glycoside System

Reservoir E — Locked Aromas

Compounds: Rutin glycoside, Quercetin sophoroside, Kaempferol forms, and Apigenin lines.[span_91](start_span)[span_91](end_span)

Behaviour: Functions like a chemical safe. Bonds can be precisely cleaved via selective β-glucosidase applications to free up aglycones and native sugars without collateral breakdown.[span_92](start_span)[span_92](end_span)

Section 09: Reservoir F — Alkaloid System

Reservoir F — Caffeine & Trigonelline

Compounds: Caffeine (7.94 g/kg DW) and Trigonelline (4.47 g/kg DW).[span_93](start_span)[span_93](end_span)

Behaviour: Caffeine stays stable; Trigonelline structural arrays begin tracking thermal shifts at 160°C+, producing rich tobacco-adjacent pyridine aromatics.[span_94](start_span)[span_94](end_span)

Section 10: Reservoir G — Chlorophyll System

Reservoir G — The Green Indicator

Compounds: Chlorophyll a/b and Pheophytins.[span_95](start_span)[span_95](end_span)

Behaviour: Useful process check. Fixed rapidly via 80–100°C fixation paths to safeguard jade liquor outputs, or transitions to olive hues via deep open oxidation environments.[span_96](start_span)[span_96](end_span)

Section 11: Reservoir H — Lignan System

Reservoir H — The Novel Finding

Compounds: Cinchonain I isomers a and b.[span_97](start_span)[span_97](end_span)

Behaviour: Newly uncovered in coffee leaf context. Displays prominent structural parallels to catechins and may blend into long aerobic microbial fermentations.[span_98](start_span)[span_98](end_span)

Section 12: Reservoir I — Sugar System [Proposed Extension]

Reservoir I — The Energy Currency

Compounds: Inferred hexose, deoxyhexose, and pentose sugars.[span_99](start_span)[span_99](end_span)

Behaviour: Acts as the metabolic fuel. Feeds reducing Maillard paths or microbial fermentations, shifting local acidity values that later adjust primary enzyme limits.[span_100](start_span)[span_100](end_span)

Section 13: Reservoir J — Lipid System [Proposed Extension]

Reservoir J — The Overlooked Pool

Compounds: Membrane lipids, glycolipids, and cuticular waxes.[span_101](start_span)[span_101](end_span)

Behaviour: Disruption triggers lipoxygenase pathways, introducing fresh C6 aldehydes that define early grassy/marine top-notes.[span_102](start_span)[span_102](end_span)

Section 14: Reservoir K — Volatile Precursor System [Proposed Extension]

Reservoir K — The Hidden Pool

Compounds: Hypothesised terpene glycosides and carotenoid sets.[span_103](start_span)[span_103](end_span)

Behaviour: Speculative layer. Carotenoid breakdown under targeted thermal pathways releases ionones, developing rose-tobacco finishes.[span_104](start_span)[span_104](end_span)

Part III — Activation Events

Physical, chemical, and biological interventions that unlock, redirect, or combine the leaf's compound pools.[span_105](start_span)[span_105](end_span)

Section 15: Activation Event A — Mechanical Disruption

Physical disruption — rolling, wringing, crushing — breaks cell walls, bringing polyphenol oxidase (PPO) and phenolic substrates into contact for the first time.[span_106](start_span)[span_106](end_span)

Section 16: Activation Event B — Water & Moisture

Water provides molecular mobility for compounds previously immobilised in dry tissue. It activates hydrolytic enzymes and creates the medium for microbial activity.[span_107](start_span)[span_107](end_span)

Section 17: Activation Event C — Oxygen & Oxidative Environment

Enzymatic oxidation is structured — PPO preferentially attacks diphenols including catechins and CGAs, producing specific quinone products below approximately 70°C.[span_108](start_span)[span_108](end_span)

Section 18: Activation Event D — Heat

Heat operates differently at different temperatures: 60–80°C inactivates enzymes; 100–150°C initiates Maillard browning; 160°C+ triggers trigonelline degradation.[span_109](start_span)[span_109](end_span)

Section 19: Activation Event E — Microbial Activity

Microorganisms produce enzymes, organic acids, and secondary metabolites inaccessible through any physical or thermal process.[span_110](start_span)[span_110](end_span)

Section 20: Activation Event F — Time: The Silent Variable

Time is the medium through which every other event operates. For Citane route mapping, time should be recorded as precisely as temperature.[span_111](start_span)[span_111](end_span)

Part IV — Layer 0: Energy Systems

Transformations are energy transfer events. This layer sits beneath activation events and gives the model its physical grounding.[span_112](start_span)[span_112](end_span)

Section 21: What Is an Energy System?

reactions do not happen unless energy is available to drive them. Energy systems supply energy to push molecules over a barrier, or lower the barrier so the reaction can proceed at ambient conditions.[span_113](start_span)[span_113](end_span)

Section 22: The Five Energy Systems

  • Thermal Energy: Supplies kinetic energy to molecules, pushing them over reaction barriers.[span_114](start_span)[span_114](end_span)
  • Enzymatic Catalysis: Lowers the activation energy barrier for specific reactions at ambient profiles.[span_115](start_span)[span_115](end_span)
  • Microbial Metabolism: Microbes harvest chemical energy to reproduce, releasing new organic components.[span_116](start_span)[span_116](end_span)
  • Oxidative Potential: Oxygen accepts electrons from reduced compounds, driven by air circulation.[span_117](start_span)[span_117](end_span)
  • Water Activity: Controls free water availability to sustain biological and hydrolytic paths.[span_118](start_span)[span_118](end_span)

Section 23: Energy Systems and Activation Events

This layered model allows a more precise diagnosis of why a processing sequence produced a particular result — and more importantly, why it failed to produce an expected one.[span_119](start_span)[span_119](end_span)

Part V — Process Domains

Section 24: Domain 1 — Oxidative Domain

Entered when the leaf is physically disrupted and exposed to air below the PPO denaturation threshold. Shifts the profile toward tea-like, honeyed territories.[span_120](start_span)[span_120](end_span)

Fresh leaf harvest → Wither (12–48 hrs) → Roll/bruise (PPO contact) → Oxidation rest (2–8 hrs) → Heat-fix (80–100°C) → Dry[span_121](start_span)[span_121](end_span)

Section 25: Domain 2 — Thermal Domain

Begins where heat becomes the primary transformation agent above 130°C, inducing Maillard browning and alkaloid shifts.[span_122](start_span)[span_122](end_span)

Dry base (<8% moisture) → Roast (130–160°C, 8–20 min) → Pyrazine onset → Rapid cooling[span_123](start_span)[span_123](end_span)

Section 26: Domain 3 — Enzymatic Domain

Uses the leaf's own enzymes during long, slow ambient withers without mechanical cell disruption.[span_124](start_span)[span_124](end_span)

Section 27: Domain 4 — Koji Domain

Introduces Aspergillus oryzae cocktails to liberate amino acid pools and sugar fractions at low temperatures.[span_125](start_span)[span_125](end_span)

Section 28: Domain 5 — Microbial Domain

Encompasses natural fermentation windows driven by yeasts and LAB to produce complex ester arrays.[span_126](start_span)[span_126](end_span)

Section 29: Domain 6 — Hybrid Domain

Deliberate multi-domain sequences where each step builds on the preceding chemical adjustments.[span_127](start_span)[span_127](end_span)

Part VII — Emergent States & Route Maps

Section 33: State Alpha — Green

Intact chlorophyll dominates. Yields clean, herbal, and highly bright green infusions.[span_131](start_span)[span_131](end_span)

Section 34: State Beta — Amber
