Engere

People, Work and Household Use
This page places Engere within the daily life of the communities recorded in the South Ethiopian field study. It separates what the research directly documents from careful interpretation based on livelihood context.

Evidence basis

What is directly documented

The Engere paper documents indigenous coffee leaf brew and Engere practices in Gofa Zone, South Ethiopia, based on household surveys, focus group discussions and observation. It records 385 households and three focus groups.

The study identifies Engere as a coffee leaf brew combined with fresh cow's milk. It records three household preparation types: simple Engere, sweetened Engere and spiced Engere.

The study also reports that Engere is used by different groups in the study area, including farming and agro-pastoral communities, and is associated by respondents with strength, recovery, lactation and medicinal use.

Important distinction: The paper documents reported use, preparation and household experience. It does not provide a clinical trial proving stamina, lactation benefit or medical treatment effect. Any discussion of why Engere may matter in daily work must therefore be presented as interpretation, not proof.
Communities and landscape

The people behind Engere

The study area sits within a region of southern Ethiopia where communities occupy different ecological zones. Nearby highland and midland areas support farming systems, while lower and warmer areas include mixed farming and livestock-oriented livelihoods.

This matters because Engere combines resources from both plant-based and animal-based household economies. Coffee leaves and herbs come from the farming and botanical side of life. Milk comes from livestock.

Documented

Household preparation

The Engere study records preparation at household level using coffee leaves, water, milk and, in some methods, sweeteners, spices and herbs.

Plausible context

Ecological exchange

Because the drink combines coffee leaf and milk, it is reasonable to read Engere as a beverage that fits mixed agricultural and livestock societies. This is an interpretation of the pattern, not a direct statement proven by the paper.

Daily labour

Daytime work and physical demand

The Engere paper does not give a full time-use diary for each community. It does, however, record use among households whose livelihoods are connected to farming, agro-pastoral life and domestic production.

Regional livelihood descriptions for parts of Gamo, Gofa and neighbouring areas describe hilly and undulating terrain, food crops such as maize, enset, sweet potato, taro, teff and yams, and income sources including butter and firewood. Such livelihood systems imply regular physical work: cultivation, harvesting, carrying, fuel gathering, animal care and household food preparation.

Highland and midland farming Field preparation, planting, harvesting, crop carrying, processing enset and other staples, collecting fuelwood. A hot milk-leaf beverage could plausibly serve as a household drink after or around labour, especially where respondents associate it with strength and recovery.
Agro-pastoral or livestock-influenced households Herding, milking, watering livestock, crop cultivation, household management and market movement. Milk availability makes Engere practical. The drink may be understood as a way of combining livestock nutrition with coffee leaf brew.

These are not claims that the study measured work output or stamina. They are reasons why Engere's reported use for strength and recovery is plausible within the daily lives of the communities described.
Interpretation

Why Engere may have remained important

1. The leaf is available

Coffee leaves can be collected from the plant without the same seasonal processing required for beans. This makes the leaf a practical household resource, especially where coffee plants are already present in farms or home surroundings.

2. Milk changes the drink

Milk adds calories, fat, protein, body and familiarity. The Engere paper reports a more favourable self-reported tolerance pattern for Engere than for plain coffee leaf brew. A cautious interpretation is that milk changes both the sensory and nutritional character of the beverage.

3. Spices and herbs make it a household system

Spiced Engere is not only leaf plus milk. It may include ginger, coriander, rue, basil, lemongrass, Ethiopian cardamom, garlic leaf, fennel, chilli and salt. This shows that Engere belongs to a broader domestic practice of combining local plants according to taste, availability and perceived usefulness.

4. It fits working households

A beverage that combines hot liquid, plant extract, milk and optional spices is well suited to communities whose lives include manual labour, livestock care, farming and household recovery needs. This does not prove a physiological effect, but it explains why such a drink could remain useful.

Limits

What should not be claimed

  • Do not claim that Engere clinically proves stamina, lactation support or disease treatment.
  • Do not claim a defined trade route for coffee leaves unless a source documents it.
  • Do not claim that one community invented Engere unless the paper explicitly establishes that history.
  • Do not claim that milk scientifically neutralizes all risks. The paper reports household experience, not a controlled safety trial.
  • Do not present Engere as a pseudo-herbal product. It is better understood as a documented household beverage tradition.
Best framing for BUNA: Engere shows that coffee leaves were not merely botanical material. In South Ethiopian households, they could become part of a milk-based beverage system connected to work, care, nourishment and local plant knowledge.
