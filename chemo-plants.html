The botanical cast of Chemo

Meet the Plants

Ten documented ingredients. Each with a role, a name, and a reason for being in the cup. Tap any plant to learn more.

Coffee Leaf Buna Kitel
Coffea arabica L. The base
›

The leaf that makes Chemo possible. Not the bean — the leaf. Young tender tips from the terminal branches of the coffee plant are preferred: brightest green, highest in aromatic oils, most responsive to roasting. Mature leaves work but produce a less vivid brew. The leaf is always fully strained out before serving — it is never consumed.

Documented quantity
164g fresh leaves per litre of water · or 39g dried
Flavour role
The body and bitterness of the brew. The foundation everything else builds on.
Preparation
Roasted (most common), lightly heated, or used fresh — chosen before brewing begins.
Key compounds
Mangiferin · Chlorogenic acids · Caffeine (lower than bean) · Volatile aldehydes
Basil Besobila
Ocimum basilicum L. Aromatic herb
›

One of the most consistently present herbs across all 64 documented Tepi Town households. Fresh leaves added to the brew during cooking. Sweet and herbal — the kind of aroma that immediately signals the drink is about something more complex than just leaf and water. Used fresh, not dried.

Documented quantity
~24g per litre of brew
Flavour role
Sweet, garden-fresh aromatic top note. One of the first things you smell in a cup of Chemo.
Form used
Fresh leaves. Not dried — the volatile aromatics that define Besobila dissipate quickly when dried.
In Ethiopian tradition
Used across multiple Ethiopian beverages and food preparations. Associated with wellbeing in traditional medicine.
Koseret ቆሰሪት
Lippia adoensis Hochst. ex Walp. Aromatic herb · Signature
›

The ingredient that makes Chemo unmistakably Ethiopian. Koseret has a distinctive herbal aroma unlike any common Western spice — slightly medicinal, deeply aromatic, impossible to substitute adequately. Native to Ethiopia and the Horn of Africa. Used across traditional Ethiopian beverages, spiced butter, and medicinal preparations. If one ingredient signals that you are drinking something rooted in a specific cultural place, this is it.

Documented quantity
~19g per litre of brew
Flavour role
The signature aromatic of Ethiopian beverages. Defines the drink's cultural identity as much as its flavour.
Availability note
Increasingly difficult to source outside Ethiopia and the Horn of Africa. Its reduced availability near towns is one of the documented pressures on Chemo's continuity.
Substitution
No direct substitute. Chemo made without Koseret is Chemo missing its most distinctive character. Use it if you can find it.
Lemongrass Tejisar
Cymbopogon citratus (DC.) Stapf Aromatic herb
›

Bright citrus and floral aroma. Lemongrass lifts the heavier roasted and spiced notes in Chemo — it is the freshness element in a predominantly warm and complex drink. Used at the second-highest quantity of the aromatic herbs, it is a significant flavour presence rather than a background note.

Documented quantity
~22g per litre — the highest of the three aromatic herbs
Flavour role
Citrus-floral freshness. Counterbalances the warmth of ginger, pepper, and roasted leaf.
Form used
Fresh or dried stalks. Lower sections of the stalk are bruised or chopped before adding to the brew.
Key compound
Citral — the primary volatile responsible for its lemon-like aroma. Stable under moderate heat.
Ginger Jingibil
Zingiber officinale Roscoe Root spice
›

The warming backbone of the spice blend. Ginger is the highest-quantity spice ingredient in the documented Chemo recipe — more than any individual herb. Its heat and depth are immediately present in a well-made Chemo. Associated with digestion and recovery in Ethiopian herbal tradition, consistent with its use across many traditional medicine systems globally.

Documented quantity
~18g per litre — the highest-quantity spice ingredient
Flavour role
Warm, pungent heat. The primary warming spice. Sets the overall warmth register of the drink.
Form used
Fresh or dried. Fresh gives sharper heat; dried gives deeper, more sustained warmth.
Key compounds
Gingerols (fresh) · Shogaols (dried/heated) — the warming and pungent compounds.
Ethiopian Cardamom Korerima
Aframomum corrorima (Braun) Jansen Root spice · Native
›

Not the green cardamom familiar from South Asian cooking — this is a different species entirely, native to Ethiopia and East Africa. Earthier, more complex, with a camphor-adjacent quality that green cardamom lacks. Korerima is one of the spices that most distinguishes Ethiopian beverages from anything made elsewhere. It cannot be substituted directly without changing the character of the drink.

Flavour role
Earthy warmth and complexity. Adds depth that sits below the more immediately vivid spices like ginger and pepper.
Native range
Ethiopia and East Africa. Grows in the same highland forest ecosystems as wild Coffea arabica.
Form used
Pods — cracked or lightly crushed before adding to brew. The seeds inside carry the primary aroma.
Substitution
Green cardamom can be used if Korerima is unavailable — but the character shifts significantly toward a South Asian spice profile rather than Ethiopian.
Cinnamon Qerfa
Cinnamomum verum J.Presl Bark spice
›

Used as bark — a small piece added to the brew, not ground powder. Cinnamon contributes a sustained sweet warmth that emerges slowly through the brew time. It is the background sweetness element, softening the edges of the more assertive spices. Its slow-release character means it is better at the end of a cup than at the beginning.

Flavour role
Sweet-warm background. Slow release. Softens and integrates the overall spice profile.
Form used
Bark — one small piece per litre. Bark releases flavour more slowly and gently than ground cinnamon.
Key compound
Cinnamaldehyde — responsible for the characteristic sweet-spicy aroma. Stable under brewing temperatures.
Note
True cinnamon (Ceylon) has a more delicate character than cassia. Either works in this context, though the flavour intensities differ.
Black Pepper Kundo berbere
Piper nigrum L. Flavour modifier
›

Immediate sharpness and bite. Black pepper is the quickest-acting of the heat elements in the Chemo spice blend — its piperine compounds are fast-release in hot water. Used alongside chilli for layered heat: the pepper hits first, the chilli sustains. Present in most documented households. Whole peppercorns lightly cracked rather than ground.

Flavour role
Immediate sharp heat. Cuts through the richness of the brew. The first heat you notice in a cup of Chemo.
Form used
Whole peppercorns, lightly cracked. 6–8 per litre documented.
Key compound
Piperine — fast-release in hot water. Also enhances absorption of other compounds.
In the blend
Works in combination with chilli — the two create a layered heat experience rather than a single sharp note.
Bird's Eye Chilli Mitmita
Capsicum frutescens L. Flavour modifier
›

Sustained heat that builds and lingers. Where black pepper hits immediately and dissipates, chilli's capsaicin compounds build slowly and stay. The two used together in Chemo create a heat experience with two distinct phases — immediate sharpness followed by sustained warmth. Used dried, in small quantities — 1–2 per litre is sufficient to be present without dominating.

Flavour role
Sustained warmth. The second phase of heat in combination with black pepper.
Form used
Dried. 1–2 whole dried chillies per litre — not ground.
Key compound
Capsaicin — binds to heat receptors for sustained effect. Fat-soluble — in the absence of fat, the heat is cleaner and more immediate.
Adjustment
1 chilli = present but gentle. 2 chillies = noticeable heat. More than 2 = becomes a defining character of the drink.
Salt Chew
Sodium chloride Flavour modifier
›

Perhaps the most surprising entry in the Chemo spice list — and yet one of the most scientifically explicable. Salt suppresses bitterness by blocking bitter taste receptors and simultaneously amplifies aromatic and sweet compounds. Its presence in Chemo is consistent with its use in traditional leaf beverage preparations across multiple cultures globally, including Engere, Kuti, and Kawa Daun. 8g per litre is the documented household average.

Documented quantity
~8g per litre of brew
Flavour role
Bitterness suppressor. Aromatic amplifier. Rounds and integrates the overall flavour without being detectably salty.
Why it works
Sodium ions block bitter taste receptor signalling. At 8g/litre the effect is flavour-balancing rather than producing a noticeably salty taste.
Cross-tradition note
Salt is documented in Engere, Chemo, Kuti and Kawa Daun — all four major Ethiopian and Indonesian coffee leaf traditions. An independent convergence on the same flavour tool.
